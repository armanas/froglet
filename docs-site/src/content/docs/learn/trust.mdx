## What is trusted?

| Trusted | Not trusted |
|---------|-------------|
| Cryptographic signatures | Marketplaces (can be wrong, cannot forge) |
| SHA-256 hashes | Transport (can fail, cannot alter signed data) |
| The deal chain | Storage (can lose data, cannot alter signed data) |
| **Staked value** | Receipt history alone (can be self-dealt) |

## Trust from stake

Trust is **not** computed from deal history. It is the total non-refundable value a provider has deposited into their identity.

$$T(F^P) = \text{total\_staked\_msat}$$

A provider stakes real money into their identity through the marketplace. This stake is:
- **Non-refundable** — already spent, cannot be recovered
- **Public** — any requester can query how much a provider has staked
- **Toppable** — providers can add more stake at any time via `marketplace.topup`
- **Destroyed on cheat** — if the provider cheats, the identity becomes worthless and the stake must be repaid to rebuild

### The requester's calculation

A requester sees a deal priced at **V** sats and a provider with stake **S** sats.

For the provider, cheating gains at most V but destroys an identity worth S (which must be repaid to re-enter the market).

$$\text{If } \frac{S}{V} > k, \text{ cheating is irrational}$$

The requester sets their own risk threshold **k** (e.g., 10). A deal is safe when the provider's stake exceeds **k** times the deal value.

## Why self-dealing does not work

In a receipt-based trust model, a provider can create fake requesters and deal with themselves to inflate their score. The cost is just Lightning routing fees — essentially free.

In staked reputation, **self-dealing does not change T.** Only paying real, non-refundable value into the identity changes T. You cannot fake stake.

## The 1% growth mechanism

A new provider entering the market can allocate 100% of margin to stake growth:

| Period | Revenue | Stake (at 1%) | Safety ratio for 8-sat deals |
|--------|---------|---------------|------------------------------|
| Week 1 | 500 sats | 5 sats | 0.6x (not yet safe) |
| Month 1 | 8,000 sats | 80 sats | 10x (threshold) |
| Month 3 | 50,000 sats | 500 sats | 62x (very safe) |
| Year 1 | 400,000 sats | 4,000 sats | 500x (dominant provider) |

An aggressive new entrant who stakes **100% of margin** grows T faster than established providers. This is a feature — it rewards commitment over incumbency.

## Game theory

### The payoff matrix

| Strategy | This deal | Reputation | Future income |
|----------|-----------|------------|---------------|
| **Honest** | +V (full price) | preserved | continues |
| **Cheat** | +base_fee only | destroyed (must re-stake S) | zero until rebuilt |

### Nash equilibrium

For any provider with meaningful stake:

$$\text{gain\_from\_cheat} = \text{base\_fee} \ll S + \text{future\_earnings}(S) = \text{cost\_of\_cheat}$$

**Honesty is the dominant strategy.** Not because the protocol enforces it — there is no enforcement. But because the economics make honesty the cheapest option.

## Quality as a separate concern

Stake proves **commitment**, not **competence**. A provider can stake 100,000 sats and still have buggy infrastructure.

Quality monitoring is a **marketplace service** (ranker), not a protocol mechanism. The marketplace can track execution success rates from receipts and surface quality rankings alongside stake amounts. Providers with high stake but poor quality get fewer deals — market competition handles the rest.

| Layer | Responsibility |
|-------|---------------|
| **Protocol** | Identity, artifacts, deals, settlement, stake |
| **Marketplace: staking** | Accept and publish non-refundable identity deposits |
| **Marketplace: ranker** | Monitor quality, flag poor performers, rank search results |

:::tip[Key insight]
The protocol guarantees that cheating is economically irrational (via stake). The marketplace guarantees that incompetence is visible (via ranking). These are different concerns with different solutions.
:::
